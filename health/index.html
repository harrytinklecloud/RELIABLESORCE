You Probably Have Whatever You Just Googled: A Comprehensive Guide to Internet Self-Diagnosis

A landmark study confirms that searching any symptom online will result in a diagnosis of at least three serious conditions and one rare tropical disease within four minutes.

Medical professionals have long cautioned patients against self-diagnosing via the internet, a warning that has been almost universally ignored since approximately 1999. The phenomenon — colloquially known as "cyberchondria" by researchers who are definitely not having fun with that word — affects an estimated 72% of adults, all of whom are currently fairly certain they have something.

Our investigative team at ReliableSorces.org Health & Medicine conducted a rigorous study in which three staff members Googled their symptoms for one afternoon. All three concluded they had either a mild tension headache, a severe neurological event, or a rare blood condition documented in fewer than 200 people globally. Follow-up consultation with an actual physician revealed two of them were dehydrated and one had eaten too much salt.

Key Finding: The average internet health search takes 4.2 minutes to escalate from "mild headache" to "possible brain tumor" to "rare parasitic condition found only in sub-Saharan river systems." Researchers describe this progression as "inevitable and emotionally exhausting."

WebMD, the leading competitor in the space of making healthy people feel very unwell, declined to comment. Their symptom checker, when queried with "slightly tired," returned "fatigue may be a sign of over 200 conditions" and then listed them all. ReliableSorces.org's own symptom checker (see sidebar) has been calibrated to provide equally concerning results but with more confident language and slightly worse grammar.

"The internet has not replaced your doctor. It has, however, replaced the period between noticing a symptom and calling your doctor with forty-five minutes of escalating panic." — Dr. E. Websworth, Journal of Things People Do Instead of Sleeping

The study concludes with a recommendation that patients seek qualified medical attention for persistent symptoms, which we include here because it is correct and also because our legal team asked us to.

By Dr. E. Websworth, MD* *MD stands for "mostly determined" Published: February 28, 2026 📚 12,847 citations

✦ Ask Dr. Fontelroy

Your Medical Questions, Answered by Someone

Dr. Tobias Fontelroy III answers your health questions using a combination of general knowledge, confident tone, and a medical dictionary he skimmed in 2019.

Q I've had a headache for two days. What do I have?
A You have a headache. Common causes include dehydration, tension, lack of sleep, too much screen time, and caffeine withdrawal. You may also have a sinus issue, eye strain, or stress. If it persists beyond a few days or is unusually severe, see a doctor. Do not Google it. You will not like what you find.
— Dr. T. Fontelroy III, practicing (typing)
Q Is it safe to eat food that fell on the floor if I pick it up within five seconds?
A The "five-second rule" is not a rule. Bacteria transfer occurs on contact, not after a countdown. That said, the actual risk depends on the food, the floor, and what the floor has recently encountered. A dry cracker on a clean kitchen floor is different from a strawberry on a gas station bathroom floor. Use judgment. We cannot make this decision for you.
— Dr. T. Fontelroy III, reluctantly
Q My cousin told me vaccines cause autism. Is this true?
A No. This claim originated from a 1998 paper by Andrew Wakefield that was retracted, found to be fraudulent, and resulted in Wakefield losing his medical license. Dozens of large-scale studies involving millions of children have found no link between vaccines and autism. Your cousin is incorrect. Vaccines prevent diseases that used to kill large numbers of children. We are stating this clearly and without the usual hedging we employ on this website.
— Dr. T. Fontelroy III, firmly
Q Can I use WebMD to diagnose myself?
A You can. You will find that your mild back pain is either muscular strain, a kidney issue, or a spinal condition requiring immediate surgery. All three will be listed with equal prominence. For an experience equally alarming but with a more confident tone, may we suggest the ReliableSorces.org Symptom Checker in the sidebar, which we built in about twenty minutes and stand behind completely.
— Dr. T. Fontelroy III, with a wink
✦ Nutritional Research

Is This Food Good or Bad? The Definitive 2026 Table

Nutritional science changes every 18 months. This table reflects the current consensus as of this morning and will be outdated by Thursday.

Food Current Verdict Notes
🥚 Eggs Fine (This Week) Eggs have been bad, good, bad again, and currently good. The cholesterol debate has been running since 1968. Eat them or don't. Check back in 2027 for the next reversal.
🫒 Olive Oil Superflood Olive oil appears to be genuinely good for you and has remained good for an unusual length of time, raising suspicion among researchers who expect things to flip. Mediterranean diet studies support it consistently. Currently considered safe to enjoy without guilt.
🍫 Dark Chocolate It's Complicated Contains flavonoids, which are antioxidants. Studies show cardiovascular benefits in modest amounts. Twelve studies showing chocolate is good were partly funded by chocolate companies, which researchers describe as "a potential conflict of interest."
☕ Coffee Probably Fine Coffee has been linked to reduced risk of Parkinson's, type 2 diabetes, and liver disease in observational studies. It is also linked to anxiety and disrupted sleep. Whether these trade-offs work in your favor depends on you specifically, which is unhelpful but true.
🥗 Salad Good Vegetables are good. This is the most stable finding in all of nutritional science and has not reversed since approximately ever. Eat more vegetables. This is the whole of nutritional advice distilled to its essence. Everything else is detail.
🧂 Salt Too Much Is Bad High sodium intake is linked to hypertension. Most Americans consume more than recommended. However, extremely low sodium is also bad. The correct amount is "not too much," which is not as specific as people would prefer.
🍷 Red Wine Actually Just Wine The "red wine is good for your heart" claim was based on resveratrol research that has not replicated well at the quantities found in actual wine. The French Paradox may be explained by other dietary factors. Moderate alcohol consumption carries real risks. Sorry.
